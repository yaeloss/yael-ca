By Yaël Ossowski | [Watchdog.org](http://watchdog.org/124780/government-spying-blame-reagan/)

American uproar over revelations of mass government spying has provoked a significant revision of the actions of U.S. spy agencies across the board.

![](images/reagan.jpg "President Ronald Reagan")

While ire has been focused at the **Obama** and **Bush** administrations, such snooping was given the green light by none other than former President **Ronald Reagan** over 30 years ago.

As [revealed in testimony](http://c-spanvideo.org/clip/4482072) during last week’s Senate Judiciary Hearing featuring members of President **Barack Obama**’s **Review Group on Intelligence and Communications Technologies**, a big share of the data collected by the government on its own citizens is authorized by [Executive Order 12333](http://www.archives.gov/federal-register/codification/executive-order/12333.html#part1), ushered in at the height of the **Cold War** in 1981 by Reagan.

“There was a great deal of metadata collected by the national security letter program. And we do speak to that in the recommendations,” said former counterterrorism official **Richard Clarke**, a member of the president’s review group [that put forth recommendations](http://www.judiciary.senate.gov/pdf/ReviewGroupReport.pdf) aimed at restricting intelligence agencies’ capacity to collect information on American citizens.

“There was also a great deal of communications-related information collected under the executive order 12333,” he added. “Public attention is focused on 215, but 215 produces a small percentage of the overall data that’s collected.”

![](images/clarke-1024x528.png "Richard Clarke")

Clarke refers to **Section 215** of the **2001 PATRIOT Act**, which [authorized the mass collection of telephone metadata](https://www.fas.org/irp/news/2013/06/nsa-sect215.pdf) in an effort to combat terrorism, the law which has received the most scrutiny of the nation’s data-collecting initiatives on its own citizens.

The executive order in question granted the government the authority to collect data and information on Americans as long as it adhered to certain stipulations.

“Agencies within the Intelligence Community are authorized to collect, retain or disseminate information concerning United States persons only in accordance with procedures established by the head of the agency concerned and approved by the Attorney General,” states the executive order.

It was amended by President **George W. Bush** in 2004 and 2008, giving additional authority to the **Director of National Intelligence** to [collect data on persons](http://georgewbush-whitehouse.archives.gov/news/releases/2008/07/20080731-2.html) tied to threats from weapons of mass destruction, international terrorism and corporate espionage.

[![sociopol_globalization06](images/sociopol_globalization06.gif)](http://watchdog.wpengine.netdna-cdn.com/wp-content/blogs.dir/1/files/2014/01/sociopol_globalization06.gif)

The order was explored at length in the review group’s report, [released in Dec. 2013](http://www.judiciary.senate.gov/pdf/ReviewGroupReport.pdf) and subsequently reviewed by members of the **U.S. Senate.**

“Executive Order 12333 specifies the missions and authorities of each element of the Intelligence Community; sets forth the principles designed to strike an appropriate balance between the acquisition of information and the protection of personal privacy; and governs the collection, retention, and dissemination of information about United States Persons (American citizens and noncitizens who are legal residents of the United States),” states the report.

If the recent documents released by **National Security Agency** leaker **Edward Snowden** [are to be believed](http://apps.washingtonpost.com/g/page/world/what-is-fascia/637/), this executive order is what specifically authorized the program allegedly collecting over 5 billion records of cell phone geo-locations per day.

The database, [known as **FASCIA**](http://apps.washingtonpost.com/g/page/world/what-is-fascia/637/), allegedly contains years of tracking data of Americans’ cell phone data, matched by cell towers and specific coordinates and collected by the NSA.

![](images/nsa-300x294.jpeg "NSA")

The NSA has denied the program is aimed at Americans.

“The Agency’s EO 12333 collection is outward-facing,” an NSA spokesperson told[The Hill newspaper](http://thehill.com/blogs/hillicon-valley/192294-nsa-uses-executive-order-to-track-phone-locations) last month. “We are not intentionally acquiring domestic information through this capability…the agency does not know and cannot track the location of every cell phone.”

The spokesperson, however, did admit the agency collects cell phone tracking for certain targets.

“This capability has been used in some of the most dangerous parts of the world, including war zones, where terrorists are actively planning to do harm to the nation,” she [told The Hill](http://thehill.com/blogs/hillicon-valley/192294-nsa-uses-executive-order-to-track-phone-locations).

The same executive order has received some publicity over the years for its [ban on international assassinations](http://www.washingtonpost.com/wp-srv/inatl/longterm/iraq/keyplayers/saddam021598.htm), a protocol breached by the Obama administration with the assassination of the U.S.-born Islamic cleric **Anwar al-Awlaki**, killed by an American drone in Sept. 2011 in the desert of Yemen. Two weeks later, a similar drone strike killed his 16 year-old son, **Abdulrahman al-Awlaki**, as well as **Samir Khan**, both U.S. citizens living in Yemen.

Obama confirmed the assassinations in a [May 2013 speech at **National Defense University**](http://www.whitehouse.gov/the-press-office/2013/05/23/remarks-president-national-defense-university) in Washington, D.C., indicating the victims went abroad to “wage war against America,” and their citizenship did not “shield” them from punishment.

“When a U.S. citizen goes abroad to wage war against America and is actively plotting to kill U.S. citizens, and when neither the United States, nor our partners are in a position to capture him before he carries out a plot, his citizenship should no more serve as a shield than a sniper shooting down on an innocent crowd should be protected from a SWAT team,” said Obama.

“I would have detained and prosecuted Awlaki if we captured him before he carried out a plot, but we couldn’t,” he added. “And as president, I would have been derelict in my duty had I not authorized the strike that took him out.”
